Our old knife block didn't fit in our new kitchen. The space between the countertop and the cabinets was too tight, making it almost impossible to pull a knife out without hitting the cupboards above. 

We ended up moving the knives into a drawer organizer, but the wooden block itself was still in great shape. Since I didn't want to throw out a nice piece of hardwood, I decided to turn it into a planter/flower pot for the kitchen counter.

---

## The design setup

The main challenge with using a knife block as a planter is water. Wood rot is a real risk if you put wet soil directly into the slots, and standard plant liners aren't made for deep, angled $60^\circ$ slots. To solve this, I designed custom plastic inserts that slide directly into the slots.

{{< figure-wrap
src="/pictures/2026-06-14_knife_block_pot/Knife_Block_Empty.jpg"
align="center"
width="400px"
alt="Empty wood knife block ready for inserts"
caption="The empty knife block and its angled slots."
>}}

For the design, I wanted to keep things clean. Normally, you'd design a lip or flange around the top of a 3D-printed insert so it hangs from the top edge. But the block already has a solid wood plate at the bottom of the slots. By measuring the depth of each slot, I designed the inserts to slide all the way down and rest directly on that bottom plate. This keeps the top of the plastic clean and out of sight.

I also designed drainage holes into the bottom of the inserts, along with built-in "soil catchers." The soil catchers make sure that excess water can drain through without washing the soil away with it.

## 3D Printing & Waterproofing

Since I don't own a 3D printer myself, a friend of mine helped me out with this step. He took care of the slicing and printed the inserts with extra perimeters (walls) to make them nice and strong. 

{{< figure-wrap
src="/pictures/2026-06-14_knife_block_pot/Pot_Inserts_Vertical.jpg"
align="center"
width="450px"
alt="3D printed inserts standing vertically"
caption="The custom 3D-printed inserts coated in clear coat."
>}}

If you've ever used 3D-printed parts to hold water, you know FDM prints aren't naturally watertight. The tiny gaps between layers will eventually seep water under pressure, which would ruin the wood block. To prevent this, I thoroughly coated both the inside and outside of the printed inserts with a clear varnish. The varnish fills in those micro-gaps and seals the plastic completely.

---

## The Final Assembly

After the clear coat cured, they fit perfectly. They slide right down to the bottom plate without any friction.

Now all that's left is to add some soil and plants. I'll probably go with succulents or small kitchen herbs since they don't need a lot of watering and fit nicely into the narrow slots.
